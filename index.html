My Favourite Idols

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj

Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj, born on February 19, 1630, at Shivneri Fort, was the visionary founder of the Maratha Empire and one of India’s most celebrated warrior-kings. Raised by his mother Jijabai, who instilled in him a deep sense of justice and responsibility, Shivaji displayed exceptional leadership and military skills from a young age. By sixteen, he had gathered loyal followers and began capturing forts, starting with Torna in 1645, laying the groundwork for a sovereign Maratha state. Shivaji’s reign was marked by his innovative use of guerrilla warfare, which allowed him to outmaneuver much larger Mughal and Adil Shahi forces. His strategic brilliance and relentless campaigns expanded his territory and challenged Mughal dominance in the Deccan. In 1674, Shivaji was crowned Chhatrapati at Raigad Fort, a moment that symbolized the formal establishment of the Maratha Empire and the assertion of Hindu sovereignty after centuries of foreign rule.





Savitribai Phule

Savitribai Phule (1831–1897) was a pioneering Indian educator, social reformer, and poet who played a transformative role in advancing women’s rights and education in 19th-century India. Born into the Mali community, she overcame her own initial illiteracy with the support of her husband, Jyotirao Phule, and became the first female teacher of modern India. In 1848, Savitribai and Jyotirao established the country’s first school for girls at Bhide Wada in Pune, defying deep-rooted societal prejudices against women’s education. Despite facing severe hostility—including verbal abuse and physical attacks—she persisted, ultimately helping to open 18 schools for girls and marginalized communities in the region. Beyond education, Savitribai was a champion for social justice. She co-founded the Satyashodhak Samaj in 1873 to fight caste discrimination and promote equality. Her advocacy extended to the rights of widows, campaigning for widow remarriage, and establishing shelters for destitute women and victims of sexual violence. In 1863, she founded India’s first home to prevent female infanticide, offering support to vulnerable women. Savitribai also took bold steps to counter untouchability, such as digging a well in her backyard for lower-caste communities who were denied access to public water sources.


Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj

Chhatrapati Sambhaji Maharaj (1657–1689), the eldest son of Chhatrapati Shivaji Maharaj, was the second ruler of the Maratha Empire and is remembered for his resilience, scholarship, and unwavering defiance against the Mughal Empire. Born at Purandar Fort, Sambhaji’s early life was marked by political intrigue and family rivalry, especially after the death of his mother, Saibai, which led to tensions with his stepmother Soyarabai, who sought to install her own son, Rajaram, as king. Despite these internal challenges, Sambhaji secured his succession after Shivaji’s death in 1680 by swiftly taking control of key forts and was crowned Chhatrapati at Raigad. Sambhaji’s reign was defined by constant warfare. He led campaigns against the Mughals, beginning with the attack on Burhanpur, and also fought the Siddis of Janjira, the Portuguese in Goa, and the kingdom of Mysore. Recognizing the need for modern weaponry, he forged an alliance with the British in 1684 to acquire arms. One of his notable victories was the Battle of Wai in 1687, where the Marathas defeated the Mughals but suffered the loss of their commander-in-chief, Hambirao Mohite, which weakened their position.



Rani Lakshmi Bai

Rani Lakshmi Bai, famously known as the Rani of Jhansi, stands as one of the most iconic figures of Ind ia’s First War of Independence in 1857. Born as Manikarnika Tambe on 19 November 1828 in Varanasi, she was raised in an environment that encouraged martial skills and independent thinking—traits unusual for a Brahmin girl of her era. After marrying Maharaja Gangadhar Rao of Jhansi, she became Rani Lakshmi Bai. The couple’s only biological son died in infancy; before his own death, the Maharaja adopted a young boy, Damodar Rao, as his heir. However, the British East India Company, invoking the Doctrine of Lapse, refused to recognize the adoption and annexed Jhansi, denying Lakshmi Bai’s right to rule. When the 1857 rebellion erupted, Rani Lakshmi Bai emerged as a formidable leader. She organized a 10,000-strong army, including women warriors, and became the regent of Jhansi, ruling on behalf of her adopted son. Her defiant proclamation, “Meri Jhansi nahi doongi” (“I will not surrender my Jhansi”), became a rallying cry for resistance. In March 1858, British forces under Major General Hugh Rose besieged Jhansi. Rani Lakshmi Bai led her troops with remarkable courage, personally taking part in combat, dressed in warrior’s attire and wielding weapons. After a fierce two-week battle, Jhansi fell, but Lakshmi Bai escaped with her son, later joining forces with other rebel leaders like Tatya Tope.


Chhatrapati Shahu Maharaj

Shahu Maharaj, also known as Chhatrapati Shahu Maharaj of Kolhapur, was a visionary ruler and social reformer who played a pivotal role in shaping modern Maharashtra and uplifting the marginalized sections of society. Born in 1874, he ascended the throne of Kolhapur in 1894 and ruled until his death in 1922. Shahu Maharaj is best remembered for his progressive policies aimed at social justice, especially his efforts to eradicate caste-based discrimination and promote education among the lower castes. He was the first Indian ruler to introduce reservations in government jobs and educational institutions for the backward classes, a policy that laid the foundation for affirmative action in India. A staunch advocate of education, Shahu Maharaj established numerous schools and hostels for boys and girls from oppressed communities, ensuring that financial constraints did not hinder their access to learning. He also supported the non-Brahmin movement and worked closely with social reformers like Mahatma Jyotirao Phule and Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, providing them with both moral and financial support. Shahu Maharaj’s reign saw the implementation of various reforms, including the abolition of bonded labor, encouragement of widow remarriage, and the promotion of inter-caste dining and marriages. His administration was marked by transparency, efficiency, and a commitment to the welfare of all his subjects, regardless of caste or creed.
